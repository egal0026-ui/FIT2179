AUSTRALIA Federal Election History

INTRODUCTION

On January 1st, 1901, the Federation of Australia occurred, establishing the Commonwealth of Australia and a federal Government. The federal government consists of a lower house (the House of Representatives) and an upper house (the Senate).

In 1901, during the first federal election, the House of Representatives consisted of 75 seats, representing 75 electoral divisions across the country. The political party that held the majority of seats in the House of Representatives was elected as the governing party of Australia. Once the governing party was elected, they had a maximum of 3 years to call the next federal election, allowing the governing party to hold the election at a time they feel the most confident to be reelected. Since 1901, the House of Representatives has grown to have 150 seats, with the electoral division boundaries being frequently redistributed.

Comparatively, the Senate elections run on a fixed schedule of 3 years. State senators have a fixed term of 6 years, and territory senators 3. At every election, the state senate seats are elected alternatingly, so each half of the state senators are elected every 6 years. All territory senators are elected at each election. In 1901, there were a total of 36 senators, 6 per state and 0 per territory. Nowadays, there are 12 seats per state and 2 per territory.

GOVERNING PARTIES

In the early days of the Commonwealth of Australia, there were 3 leading parties in federal elections: the Protectionist Party, the Free Trade Party, and the Australian Labor Party. Having 3 large parties proved to be difficult to govern with, especially after the 1903 election when the 3 parties achieved a fairly even split of seats, meaning that no party held a majority. This meant that it was very difficult to achieve a majority ruling on bills and the parties had to rely on alliances with other parties, which frequently collapsed due to disagreements. This resulted in the minority governing party and prime minister changing 3 times within a single election term.

In 1910, the two-party system was established between the Australian Labor Party and the Commonwealth Liberal Party. From this point on, Labor would remain as one of the major parties in the two-party system, while their opposition would change over time, until 1944 when the Liberal Coalition would be cemented as a major party to this day.

POLITICAL PARTIES

Political Parties are organisations of a collection of members that share politics and values. Political parties aim to have as many of their members elected in different seats as possible. The more members elected, the more votes a party has on government issues. Political parties rely on all of their elected members voting together. If a party or alliance of parties hold the majority of seats, they hold voting power.

Some elected members of parliament are not affiliated with a party. These members are called Independents, and can often be the deciding vote when a party does not have a majority.

There are major political parties and minor political parties. Major parties are the ones who obtain majority or near-majority seats. These parties are the ones that either contend to become the governing party or obtain a high amount of votes in the senate. Currently, the major parties of Australia are the Australian Labor Party and the Liberal Coalition parties.

Minor parties are parties that manage to gain a few seats in either the House of Representatives or the Senate. Like Independents, minor parties often act as a deciding vote and are not strictly aligned with any other party. Many minor parties focus their campaigns on specific electoral divisions that they have fostered support in, compared to major parties which tend to campaign at most electoral divisions.

The major and minor parties can be different in the House of Representatives compared to the Senate. For instance, the Australian Greens are considered a major party in the senate as they currently have 12 seats, but a minor party in the House of Representatives where they hold only 1 seat. There are also minor parties that only campaign in the House of Representatives or the Senate, but not both.

In more recent years, there has been an increase in the number of minor parties being elected. In earlier years, non-major party candidates were usually just Independents. As time has gone on, Independent candidates have begun establishing their own political parties, hence the rise in minor parties. There is also a factor of dissatisfaction with the current major parties and increased visibility of minor parties that has motivated voters to move away from major parties.

HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES

The House of Representatives is the lower house of Australia's federal parliament. In a House of Representatives election, Australian citizens vote solely for the electoral division they reside in. Within each electoral division, the candidate with majority votes is elected.

In 1901, the House of Representatives had 75 seats. In 1949, the number of seats grew to 123. Then in 1984, it grew again to 148. Between these years, the exact number of seats would increase or decrease slightly as electoral divisions were frequently adjusted to more accurately reflect the population distribution across the country.

In the most recent election in 2025 there were a total of 150 seats.

In the 2025 election, the Australian Labor Party had a landslide victory, winning a total of 94 seats. This was the highest number of seats that the party had ever won, and the highest percentage of seats since the Nationalist Party's win in 1917.

Labor won the majority of seats in all states and territories excluding Queensland. They also managed to retain all 75 seats that they had won in the 2022 election. The Liberal Coalition retained 42 of their seats but lost 14 of their seats to Labor, 2 to Independents and gained 1 from an Independent, leaving them with 43 seats.

Independents retained 8 of their seats. They lost 1 seat to the Liberal Coalition but gained 2 seats from them. The Greens lost 3 of their seats to Labor, only retaining 1.

The minor parties the Centre Alliance and Katter's Australian Party retained their only seat.

The general trend across all states is that the Liberal Coalition wins more regional or rural seats, while Labor wins more suburban or metropolitan seats. The exceptions are Northern Territory, Tasmania, and the Australian Capital Territory, in which Liberal did not win any seats.

SENATE

The Senate is the upper house in Australia's federal parliament. It consists of 76 seats, 12 for each state and 2 for each territory. In 2025, 6 out of 12 seats were elected in each state and 2 out of 2 seats were elected in each territory. Unlike the House of Representatives where each individual electorate seat has its own voting, in the Senate election, each state votes collectively, and the seats are appointed to parties in accordance with the percentage of votes they received, thus a party does not require a majority of the votes to be elected to a seat and voters do not vote for a specific candidate.

In the 2025 election, Labor was elected 16 seats, increasing their seats in the Senate by 3 to a total of 28. The Liberal National Coalition was elected to 13 seats, decreasing their seats in the Senate by 3 to a total of 27. The Greens retained all 6 of their seats, maintaining their total of 11 seats. Currently, no party holds a majority in the senate.

Over the last 2 decades, there has been an increase in minor parties or Independents obtaining seats in the Senate.

The majority of electoral divisions have a high voter turnout of over 90%. The electorate with the lowest voter turnout is Lingiari, the regional electorate of the Northern Territory, with a voter turnout of 62.74%. The electorate with the highest turnout was Hotham, a suburban electorate in Victoria, with a voter turnout of 99.58%.

CONCLUSION

Australia's federal election history is an interesting reflection of the history of the country's political shifts over time. The difference in party make-up between the House of Representatives and the Senate is indicative of differences in their voting systems. The different make-up of the lower and upper houses of parliament is important, because it ensures that a diverse variety of members of parliament can vote on passing bills. Understanding the locations of voters is also helpful in understanding Australia's political standing and make-up.
